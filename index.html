Neurological and Thermodynamic Mechanisms Underlying Anomalous Presence Perception

Kapadia, T. (2025). Version 16. Zenodo.

A unified scientific explanation for the universal human experience of sensing a deceased person's presence. Proposes four interacting mechanisms: infrasound-induced cortical arousal operating below conscious sensory threshold, predictive processing failure during bereavement, Neural Persistence — the continued firing of long-term potentiated neural pathways built around a specific individual — and Cardiac Neural Persistence, the encoding of relational memory in the heart's intrinsic nervous system. Draws on thermodynamics, neuroscience, olfactory biology, and the quantum observer analogy.

Kapadia, T. (2025). Neurological and Thermodynamic Mechanisms Underlying Anomalous Presence Perception (v16). Zenodo. https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19161509

Correspondence with Prof. Karl Friston (University College London) — who described the work as "extremely well informed."

Essay Open Access · Zenodo

Consciousness? — The Accident and Its Cost

Kapadia, T. (2026). Zenodo.

A philosophical essay examining consciousness not as a triumph of evolution but as an unresolved consequence of it. Argues that self-awareness created the problems it was supposed to solve, constructed an illusion of a self it cannot see through, and left the species that has it permanently inside the question it cannot answer. Closes on the hard problem of consciousness and proposes a structural parallel between the quantum observer collapse and the mechanism of conscious perception as a direction worth pursuing.

About

I am a 17-year-old independent researcher from India, currently completing higher secondary education. My work sits at the intersection of neuroscience, thermodynamics, and the philosophy of mind — specifically the questions that existing scientific frameworks have not yet adequately addressed.

I do not begin with literature reviews. I begin with questions that don't have satisfying answers and build mechanistic frameworks to explain them. Only afterwards do I find the science that confirms, challenges, or extends what intuition had already mapped. That sequence — curiosity first, rigour later — is not a limitation of this work. It is, perhaps, how most original questions begin.

My long-term goal is neuroscience research focused on consciousness — understanding not just the correlates of subjective experience, but what it fundamentally is and why it exists at all. Everything else is infrastructure toward that.
